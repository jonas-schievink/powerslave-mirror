INSTALLING LEDs

You can add external lighting to the Xbox.  The following information is a generlization.  You may have to do more research for specific applciations.

LED:  LED's are special diodes that emit light when connected in a circuit. They are frequently used as "pilot" lights in electronic appliances to indicate whether the circuit is closed or not. A a clear (or often colored) epoxy case enclosed the heart of an LED, the semi-conductor chip.

POLARITY: The two leads extending below the LED epoxy enclosure, or the "bulb", indicate how the LED should be connected into a circuit. The negative side of an LED lead is indicated in two ways: 1) by the flat side of the bulb, and 2) by the shorter of the two leads extending from the LED. The negative lead should be connected to the negative terminal of a battery. LED's operate at relative low voltages between about 1 and 4 volts, and draw of current is between about 10 and 40 milliamperes (mA). Voltages and currents substantially above these values can melt a LED chip.

CURRENT LIMIT RESISTOR:  You need a resistor attached to the negative side of the LED so that it will not pull more current than is required to light the LED.  LEDs have a negative temperature coefficient of resistance.  This means as the LED heats up, its resistance decreases, and it will pull more current.  Unless the flow of current is restricted in some way (such as by a current limiting resistor) the LED may simply keep drawing more and more current until it self-destructs.  The resistor helps in assuring the curent draw is not more than the LED can handle.  It also helps so assure the Xbox power supply is not being taxed when using multiple LEDs in a curcuit.

CALCULATING THE RESISTOR:  Calculating the proper resistance of a current limiting resistor is actually quite easy.  To calculate the resistance, you will need to know three things.  1. Supply voltage (Vs),  2. Operating voltage of the LED (Vf), and 3. Operating current of the LED (If).  Once these three variables are known, they can be plugged into the following formula to find the proper resistance value:

R = Vs - Vf / If

Example #1: Red LED (1.8 Vf and 0.020 If) operating from 5 VDC supply: R = (5V – 1.8V)/0.020 A = 160 Ohms.

Example #2: Blue LED (3.4 Vf and 0.020 If) operating from 12 VDC supply:  R = (12V – 3.4V)/0.020 A = 430 Ohms (470 Ohms could be substituted)

Ok, so now we know the resistance value of the current limiting resistor but what about its power rating?  Here is how to determine the power rating.

Example #3: Blue LED (3.4 Vf and 0.020 If) operating from 12 VDC supply: Voltage drop across resistor = (12V – 3.4V) = 8.6V
Power in watts = I x E = 0.020A x 8.6V = 0.172 watts

BLUE and WHITE LEDs require more voltage than Red, Green, and Yellow (Most white LEDs are actually blue LEDs that contain a phosphor coating that absorbs blue light, and emits a broad-spectrum white light).

So in this example, and just about any you are going to use for your Xbox, a 1/4 watt resistor would do fine.  (1/4 watt = 0.250 watt)

Using more than one LED in Parellel:  Each LED gets its own resistor!  One voltage supply to the positive sides of all the LEDs, then each individual negative side with resistor, then to ground (-).  Remember, your Xbox power supply is rated at so many watts!  Total up the number of LEDs you want to use, say 10.  Each LED uses 20mA (If), that's going to be 200mA of current.  Doesn't seem like much, but the Xbox's power supply does have its imits.  I doubt you would be able to use enough LEDs to kill the power supply, but it's the 5V you do not want to over-step its output. 

Using more than one LED in SERIES:  This is different then when in Parellel  If you have Six red LEDs wired in series and operating from 12 VDC supply:  (1.8V + 1.8V + 1.8V + 1.8V + 1.8V + 1.8V) = 10.8V total.  R = (12V – 10.8V)/0.020A = 60 Ohms.  This resistor is placed on the last LED in the chain, on its negative side, then the resistor to ground (-).  You normally do not want to wire in series, because if one LED blows they all go out!  Then, finding the bad LED will be next to impossible.

INSTALLING THE RESISTOR:  Of course, you are going to need to drill the hole(s) for the LED(s), and make sure there is room behind it.  You can bend the leads down/up, or cut them as needed, just remember the polarity!  Assure fitment before soldering your wires on to the LED.  The current limiting resistor can go on anywhere in the circuit, as long as it is on the NEGATIVE side of the LED.  

POWER SUPPLY FOR LED:  You can use a "Y" adapter for the Hard Disk power cable.  You would cut the oher end off and use the power from the RED (5V) or YELLOW (12V) wires.  The blacks are ground.  Make sure all exposed wires are coverfed or capped so they do not short out anywhere inside the Xbox case!  It is a lot easier than you think, just make sure you know what you are doing! 

So, have fun with your lighting.
