Some mod chips come with a bios image already on them, called the Cromwell bios. The Cromwell bios is there to allow you to be able to turn on your Xbox, and be able to flash a bios image of your choice onto the mod chip, without the need for hardware programmers.

Newer mod chips such as the Xenium, SmartXX and Xecuter3, feature their own form of operating system. This allows you to have even greater control when flashing your mod chip with a new bios, as well as having many useful features on them as well.


BIOS Banks Explained
Mod chips have a chip on them called a "flash rom". This varies in size from mod chip to mod chip. The bigger the size, the more different bios images, or more complex bios images, you can put on there. The original Xbox bios is 256kb in size, so most mod chips divide their flash rom up into multiples of 256kb. Most mod chips will allow you to have different sized bios images on the flash rom however, but they are always multiples of 256kb. For example, your mod chip may have a 1mbit flash rom on it. That would mean you could have one 1mbit bios image on there, or two 512kb bios images on there, or four 256kb bios images on there. It depends on what you want type of bios you want to use on the mod chip. Remember though, you MUST always flash a bios bank with bios image that matches its size. So if the bank is 256kb, it must be flashed with a 256kb bios image, or else it won't work properly.

Flashing Using Cromwell
If your mod chip has the Cromwell bios on it, you will be presented with a screen when you first turn your Xbox on with your mod chip installed. The Cromwell bios screen will normally wait a few moments, then prompt you to insert a CD with a new bios image on it. When you do this, it will reflash the current bios bank it is using, with the one you have put on the CD. Once it has finished flashing, it will reboot the Xbox using the new bios image. After doing that you will be able to put new applications on the Xbox, such as a replacement dashboard, or play a backed up game.

Flashing Using Onboard Operating System
Mod chips like the Xenium, SmartXX, X-Chip and Xecuter 3, all use their own onboard operating system. These are a bit like dashboards in a way. They will allow you to perform many different tasks, one of which will be flashing the mod chip. The onboard operating systems on these mod chips will ask you to locate a bios image you wish to flash with, which can be on the hard drive or on a CD. It will then flash the mod chip with the selected bios.

Troubleshooting:
A bad flash can occur at any time, and for no reason. The most common reason for it is flashing a bios bank with a bios image that is the wrong size. It can also be caused by a corrupt bios bank, or a bad connection between the mod chip and the Xbox.

Recovering With A Programmer
Most mod chips have a programmer unit that is either supplied with the mod chip when it is sold, or available seperately at a later date. Chips that have hardware programmers include: Matrix, Chameleon, Xecuter2.0-2.6.  Recovering from a bad flash with a hardware programmer is very straight forward. Simply take the chip out of the Xbox, or if it has a long enough cable, leave it in the Xbox (make sure its switched off), and connect it to your programmer. Plug the programmer into your PC (normally on the LPT/Printer Port), and load up the software from the makers of your mod chip, and flash the bank with a new bios image. It is always important to make sure you flash a bank with the correct sized bios image. Eg if the bank is 256kb, flash it with a 256kb bios image.

Recovering With A USB Programmer
Some chips feature USB support, which allows them to be flashed using your PC via a USB connection. The chips that support this to date are the X-B.I.T v1.0/1.5 and the X-Chip.
These can be reflashed by connecting the chip to the PC via a USB cable. Then load up the software from your mod chip manufacturer, and relfash the bank with a correctly sized bios image.

Recovering By Using A Different Bank
If your mod chip is setup so it's using more than one bank, you can simply switch to a bank that either still has Cromwell on it, or has a working bios image. From there you can then use software on the Xbox to reflash the bad bank. Some mod chips allow you to switch banks via software control on the Xbox, but others may require you to physically change the dip switches on the mod chip to change the bank you're flashing to.

Recovering With Another Modchip (Hot Swap)
If you have another mod chip which has a working bios image on it, you can use it to reflash your other mod chip. To do this you will need to boot up your Xbox with the working mod chip installed, then while the Xbox is turned on, you will need to install the broken mod chip. You can then use software on the Xbox to reflash the chip with a working bios image. Please note: This method is only recommended for experienced users, as it can easily cause damage to your Xbox that cannot be repaired. Only mod chips using pin headers or pogo pins should be used for this.
